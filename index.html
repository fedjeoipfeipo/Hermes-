The Beginning

A German immigrant,
a needle, and a horse.

Thierry Hermès was born in Krefeld, Germany in 1801 a city known for its silk. He arrived in Paris as a craftsman with a single skill: making the finest harnesses in France for the nobility's horses.

In 1837, he opened a small workshop near La Madeleine. His clients were Napoleon III and the crowned heads of Europe not because of marketing, but because his stitching was simply better than anyone else's. By the time his grandson Émile-Maurice inherited the house, the Tsar of Russia was placing private orders.

That obsession with craft, passed from father to son to grandson through six generations, is still the engine of a house that briefly surpassed LVMH as the world's most valuable luxury company in 2025.

1837

"The first client is the horse. The second is the rider."

Jean-Louis Dumas, CEO 1978–2006

Thierry died in 1878. His son Charles-Émile inherited not a brand, but a discipline. The obsession passed whole stitch by stitch, generation by generation through six heirs without ever being sold to a conglomerate.

187 Years in 8 Moments

From saddles
to surpassing LVMH.

1837

The Harness Workshop

Thierry Hermès opens a workshop in Paris serving European nobility. His saddle-stitch technique two needles, two threads in opposite tension remains unchanged to this day.

1880

24 Rue du Faubourg Saint-Honoré

Charles-Émile Hermès moves the house to its permanent address, where it remains today. The first retail store opens alongside the workshop.

1918

The First Zipper in France

Émile-Maurice Hermès secures exclusive zipper rights for France after crafting a leather golf jacket for the Prince of Wales. The zipper becomes known as "la fermeture Hermès" throughout the country.

1937

The Silk Carré is Born

The iconic square silk scarf debuts exactly 100 years after the house's founding. Each scarf takes 18 months and 250 silkworm cocoons to produce. Up to 750 hours just to engrave the printing screens one per color. The average scarf carries 27 distinct shades.

1942

The Accidental Orange

WWII material shortages force Hermès to accept the only available box color: bright orange "the color nobody wanted." It accidentally becomes the most recognized packaging in luxury history. The dark brown Bolduc ribbon is added in 1949, completing the look.

1956

Grace Kelly & The Bag

A Life Magazine photo of Princess Grace Kelly using a Hermès Sac à Dépêches to shield her pregnancy causes the bag to sell out worldwide overnight. Hermès renames it the Kelly.

1984

The Birkin is Born on a Flight

CEO Jean-Louis Dumas sits next to actress Jane Birkin on a Paris–London flight. Her basket spills. By landing, he has sketched the world's most valuable handbag on an airplane sickness bag. The finished prototype is delivered to Jane in 1985 a detail the mythology often swallows.

2024

€15.2 Billion. The Luxury Market Goes Quiet.

While LVMH and Kering struggle through a luxury slowdown, Hermès grows 15% and briefly overtakes LVMH in market cap becoming the most valuable luxury house in history. Operating margin: 40.5%. The same as Apple's. A craft atelier. Performing like a tech company.

The Color That Wasn't Chosen

Iconic
by accident.

In 1942, Nazi-occupied Paris was rationing everything including cardboard. Hermès ran out of its signature cream colored boxes. Their supplier offered the only color no one else wanted: a vivid orange.

Émile-Maurice Hermès accepted it out of necessity. The result was "iconic by accident" the brand's own words. Seven years later, in 1949, he added the dark brown Bolduc ribbon that completed the look the world now recognizes instantly.

188 different sizes of the orange box exist one for every product category

The exact shade has no Pantone equivalent it exists only as "Hermès Orange"

The EU refused to let Hermès trademark the color in 2005, calling it "too common." The box remains more recognizable than most logos

Keeping the original box increases a bag's resale value by thousands of dollars

The Symbol

A painting acquired in the 1920s
still running the show.

In the 1920s, Émile-Maurice Hermès acquired a graphite drawing by French painter Alfred de Dreux titled "Le Duc Attelé, Groom à L'Attente" a hitched carriage, waiting for its master.

The logo debuted officially in 1945. Notice what's missing: there is no driver in the carriage. This is intentional. Hermès delivers the craft. You the owner complete the story. An 80 year old logo carrying a 187 year old idea.

And it doesn't appear on every product. The deliberate absence on certain pieces is itself the signal you don't need to announce what you are when everyone already knows.

The Psychology

Most luxury brands scream their logo. Hermès whispers and the silence costs more.

Five Tactics, One Philosophy

The psychology of
deliberate refusal.

01
You don't buy a Birkin. They allow you to buy one.

You cannot walk into a Hermès boutique and request a Birkin. You must first build a "purchase history" spending thousands on scarves, belts, and accessories. Then, and only then, a sales associate may offer you the opportunity to buy one. You can purchase a maximum of two bags per year. This is not a waitlist. This is an audition. The psychological effect is the inverse of all retail logic: the harder something is to acquire, the more proof it becomes of your status. Hermès weaponizes rejection as the ultimate marketing tool. The Birkin's secondary market price $22,000 to $30,000 for a bag retailing at $13,500 exists entirely because of this system.

02
Prices go up during crises.

When COVID hit in 2020, luxury brands panicked. Hermès raised prices. The Birkin 25 has increased from $9,400 in 2016 to $13,500 in 2026 a 44% rise over a decade while inflation was far lower. Every price increase makes existing owners wealthier on paper and makes aspiring owners more desperate. It's a self-reinforcing spiral of desire engineered through pricing alone.

03
"We do not do marketing."

Axel Dumas, Hermès CEO, stated publicly: "We do not do marketing, we do not try to create an illusion by paying people to wear our products." No celebrity ambassadors. No paid influencers. No Super Bowl ads. The craft is the campaign. And when the CEO says this to the press, it becomes the most powerful marketing statement in luxury because it proves the brand doesn't need to try.

04
The Birkin as financial asset.

Between 1984 and 2015, Birkin bags outperformed gold and the S&P 500 with an average annual return of 14.2%. In July 2025, Jane Birkin's original prototype sold at Sotheby's for $10.1 million the most expensive handbag ever auctioned. This repositioned the product from fashion to investment thesis, attracting an entirely new class of buyer: wealth managers, collectors, and funds treating Birkins as an asset class.

05
6 generations. Zero outside investors.

When LVMH's Bernard Arnault secretly accumulated a 23% stake in Hermès between 2010 and 2013 in an attempted hostile takeover, the Hermès family united 52 members under a holding company called H51, collectively owning 50.2% of capital. They repelled the largest luxury conglomerate in the world. Family ownership is not a legacy choice it's the structural guarantee that no quarterly earnings call will ever compromise a 187 year old craft philosophy.

The Silk Carré

18 months.
250 cocoons.
One scarf.

The Hermès silk scarf the carré has been made the same way since 1937. Each one begins with silkworms on Hermès-owned farms in Brazil. A single 90cm scarf requires 450km of silk thread and 250 mulberry moth cocoons.

The design process alone takes 18 months. Engraving the printing screens requires up to 750 hours of work one screen per color, up to 40 colors per scarf. Every hem is hand rolled and hand-stitched by artisans in Lyon, France.

Step 01

Artist commissioned. Design conceptualized over months around an annual theme.

Step 02

600+ hours of hand tracing the design digitally, stroke by stroke.

Step 03

Engravers create one printing screen per color up to 40 screens, taking 750 hours.

Step 04

Screen printing on 100 meter tables. Colors applied lightest to darkest.

Step 05

Each scarf hand-cut, washed, ironed, and inspected individually.

Step 06

Hems hand-rolled and stitched by a seamstress 40 minutes per scarf, minimum.

Step 07

Any scarf with color slippage is destroyed. No exceptions. No seconds market.

The Investment Thesis
"A Birkin outperformed gold and the S&P 500 for 31 years straight."

Between 1984 and 2015, Birkin bags delivered an average annual return of 14.2% beating both gold (1.9%) and the S&P 500 (11.66%) over the same period.

At Sotheby's, the average resale price for a classic Birkin 30 in 2025 was $22,300 a 2.4x premium over retail. Pristine examples in rare colors regularly trade between $28,000 and $30,000.

In July 2025, Jane Birkin's original prototype the bag Dumas sketched on that flight in 1984 and delivered to her in 1985 sold for $10.1 million at Sotheby's Paris. The most expensive handbag ever sold at auction.
